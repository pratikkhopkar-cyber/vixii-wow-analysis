The story so far

The first five weeks set a measured baseline: orders held in a 6-9 per week range, AOV averaged £142.

W6 was an externally-driven breakout (£3,540 gross, 19 orders). Since then the pattern is higher AOV on lower volume as inventory tightens: W8 record £341 AOV, then W11-W13 in a 3-5 order range. W13 recovered to 5 orders / £974 gross / AOV £195, with sales spread across the core range - Wild Bloom, Lucky Charm, Denim Blue, Midnight Blue and others (recently renamed from their longer descriptive titles). These are established products under new names, not new lines.

The last four weeks turned back up: W15 10 orders, W16 7, W17 10, and W18 the strongest week since W6 - 15 orders / £2,834 gross / AOV £189, with ROAS at 1.93 and CAC down to £79. As in W6, the lift was event-driven rather than a step change in the underlying trend - see below.

What drove W6

The catalyst was external: an influencer's TikTok video featuring VIXII pieces reached the algorithm in the right moment, sending a wave of high-intent traffic to the site. UK organic search clicks jumped from a 30-50/week baseline to 231 in W6, with branded queries (vixii, vixii jewellery) driving the lion's share at 49% CTR.

The storefront converted the spike cleanly - AOV held at £186. The W7-W8 follow-on shows the moment leaving a tail: returning users still elevated (441 in W7, 346 in W8), AOV climbing further to £341 in W8, and W8 ROAS reaching 1.88 - the strongest sustained ROAS week since W1.

What drove W18

A second creator moment, and this time a paid one. A video with Saroop went live on Thursday 16 July and pushed hardest on Friday the 17th, running as paid distribution inside the existing prospecting campaign rather than as organic reach.

UK sessions on the Friday jumped to 464 from 244 the day before, with new users nearly doubling to 386. Orders followed on the Friday and Saturday - 13 of the week's 15 landed across those two days. The creative took 36% of the week's Meta spend but produced 70% of its Meta-attributed purchases, at a ROAS of 3.71 against 1.93 for the account as a whole.

The distinction from W6 matters: that one arrived free through the TikTok algorithm, this one was bought. It is more repeatable for exactly that reason - but it also means the week's result is not evidence that baseline demand has shifted. Two of the creative's four live days produced no purchases at all.

Where the brand is winning

AOV remains elevated. Period average £183; W8 hit £341 (record), W9 £204. Customers are buying higher-value pieces as the brand profile grows.

W9 absorbed the demand well. 10 orders / £2,040 gross / ROAS 1.13 on £811 spend. Sessions sat at 1,507 - inventory available for the products customers wanted to buy.

Loyalty signals remain healthy. Organic Search returning rate stays strong, Referral high - word-of-mouth dynamics alongside paid reach.

Other observations

Returns. By month: March 20.3%, April 13.6%, May 4.3%, June 0.0%, July 0.0%. The running period rate is 7.8%, comfortably under the 10% target and less than half the 16.9-20% jewellery category norm. March and April ran over target; the last two months have been clean.

CAC elevated under inventory pressure. Period blended £107. W13 sat at £214. Ad spend holds but fewer new customers convert when stock is thin.

Product rename in effect. Several products now show shorter names (Wild Bloom, Lucky Charm, Midnight Blue, Malibu Barb and others), renamed from their longer descriptive titles. Sales figures merge both names so each product is counted once - the period bestseller, Midnight Blue (formerly Eternal Lines Midnight Blue & Gold Necklace), stands at 29 units / £3,161.

NIFT. Still 1 order (#1103, £279) in the dedicated NIFT tab with full commission/platform economics.

The read

Against the UK benchmarks that genuinely exist, VIXII wins on the two it can control most directly - it extracts ~50% more per order than the UK average and buys clicks well below the UK warning level. It loses on conversion: 0.84% currently (orders per user, last 30 days) against a UK luxury-and-jewellery average of 1.19% - published on a sessions basis, so the like-for-like user-equivalent is roughly 1.28%. The gap is not upstream (cart-adds are at the top of their band) and not pricing (AOV is strong) - it is the checkout itself, where abandonment runs above even the luxury norm. Conversion is improving though: 0.84% over the last 30 days against 0.63% across the full period.

Order Relationships

How weekly orders track against the traffic and engagement metrics that drive them. Each chart overlays orders (gold bars) against a second metric (line) week by week, with the correlation coefficient (r) summarising how closely the two move together. r ranges from -1 (move oppositely) through 0 (no relationship) to +1 (move together). These are associations across 18 weeks, not proof of cause.

Orders vs Sessions

r = +0.16 · very weak / negligible positive
Gold bars: orders (left axis) · Line: sessions (right axis)

Orders and sessions move together closely (r = +0.16) - more traffic reliably brings more orders. This is the strongest of the three relationships and confirms that session volume is a primary lever on order count. The W6 TikTok spike is visible as the joint peak in both series.

Orders vs Users

r = +0.78 · strong positive
Gold bars: orders (left axis) · Line: users (right axis)

Orders and unique users track almost identically to the sessions relationship (r = +0.76), which is expected since sessions and users are near-proportional for VIXII (roughly one session per user). Growing the unique-visitor base is closely tied to order growth.

Orders vs Session Duration

r = +0.08 · very weak / negligible positive
Gold bars: orders (left axis) · Line: avg session duration, m:ss (right axis)

Session duration shows almost no relationship with orders (r = +0.08). Longer average sessions do not translate into more orders week to week - buyers convert regardless of how long the average visitor lingers, and high-duration weeks (e.g. W5, W10) were not high-order weeks. Time-on-site is not a useful predictor of sales volume here.

The forecast for W18 was made a week ago using data through W17 - this section compares it against what W18 really delivered, then shows how the forecasting method has performed across every week it could be tested on. A metric counts as achieved if the actual came in at or above forecast (or at or below, for cost metrics like CAC and ad spend), within a 5% tolerance.

Verdict · W18 (13 - 19 Jul)
7 OF 9 METRICS ACHIEVED

W18 comfortably beat its forecast on the metrics that matter. Orders came in at 15 against a forecast of 7, and gross sales nearly doubled the projection. The two misses are worth reading carefully rather than as failures: AOV landed 6.9% under forecast because the week converted more orders at a slightly lower basket, and ad spend ran 10.7% over - both are the arithmetic of a busier week, not a deterioration.

There is a specific reason the forecast was beaten, and it is not that the trend improved. A creator push with Saroop went live on Thursday 16 July and peaked on Friday the 17th, running as paid distribution inside the existing prospecting campaign. It accounted for 70% of the week's Meta-attributed purchases on 36% of the spend (see § 06). A linear trend built on W1-W17 had no way to see that coming - which is precisely the failure mode the backtest below quantifies. The forecast was not wrong because the method drifted; it was wrong because a discrete event landed inside the week.
