The story so far

The first five weeks set a measured baseline: orders held in a 6-9 per week range, AOV averaged £130.

W6 was an externally-driven breakout (£3,540 gross, 19 orders). Since then the pattern is higher AOV on lower volume as inventory tightens: W8 record £341 AOV, then W11-W13 in a 3-5 order range. W13 recovered to 5 orders / £974 gross / AOV £195, with sales spread across the core range - Wild Bloom, Lucky Charm, Denim Blue, Midnight Blue and others (recently renamed from their longer descriptive titles). These are established products under new names, not new lines.

What drove W6

The catalyst was external: an influencer's TikTok video featuring VIXII pieces reached the algorithm in the right moment, sending a wave of high-intent traffic to the site. UK organic search clicks jumped from a 30-50/week baseline to 231 in W6, with branded queries (vixii, vixii jewellery) driving the lion's share at 49% CTR.

The storefront converted the spike cleanly - AOV held at £181. The W7-W8 follow-on shows the moment leaving a tail: returning users still elevated (441 in W7, 351 in W8), AOV climbing further to £341 in W8, and W8 ROAS reaching 1.88 - the strongest sustained ROAS week since W1.

Where the brand is winning

AOV remains elevated. Period average £175; W8 hit £341 (record), W9 £204. Customers are buying higher-value pieces as the brand profile grows.

W9 absorbed the demand well. 10 orders / £2,040 gross / ROAS 1.07 on £811 spend. Sessions sat at 1,501 - inventory available for the products customers wanted to buy.

Loyalty signals remain healthy. Organic Search returning rate stays strong, Referral high - word-of-mouth dynamics alongside paid reach.

Other observations

Returns. March 20.2%, April 13.5% (both over target); May 0%, June (1-14) 0%. Running period rate now 8.8%, back under the 10% target after a strong W15. Partial refunds tracked separately.

CAC elevated under inventory pressure. Period blended £109. W13 sat at £214. Ad spend holds but fewer new customers convert when stock is thin.

Product rename in effect. Several products now show shorter names (Wild Bloom, Lucky Charm, Midnight Blue, Malibu Barb and others), renamed from their longer descriptive titles. Sales figures merge both names so each product is counted once - the period bestseller, Midnight Blue (formerly Eternal Lines Midnight Blue & Gold Necklace), stands at 29 units / £3,161.

NIFT. Still 1 order (#1103, £279) in the dedicated NIFT tab with full commission/platform economics.

Industry Benchmarks

How VIXII stands against demi-fine jewellery industry benchmarks for 2025-26. Demi-fine sits between fashion jewellery (high volume, low AOV) and fine jewellery (low volume, high AOV) - typically gold vermeil, 14k solid gold, or sterling silver with semi-precious stones. Where a demi-fine-specific figure exists it is used; otherwise the broader luxury-and-jewellery category benchmark is shown. USD figures use ~1.27 USD/GBP for comparison against (mostly USD) industry data; rate-based metrics (conversion, returns, ROAS, bounce) are currency-neutral.

Where VIXII leads
AOV (top of demi-fine range), Return Rate (high-performer, <10%), Bounce Rate (better than category)
Where VIXII trails
Conversion Rate (below demi-fine band), CAC (above range), ROAS (below jewelry norm) - all partly downstream of the inventory constraint
The read
VIXII's economics per order are strong (high AOV, low returns). The gap is funnel throughput - getting more of the engaged traffic to convert, which inventory recovery should directly help.

Category-wide structural challenge

Jewelry has the highest cart-abandonment rate of any retail category (81.4%) - driven by high price points, trust friction, and the tactile nature of the product. VIXII's funnel (cart-add to checkout to purchase, see §04) should be read against this backdrop: heavy mid-funnel drop-off is the category norm, not a VIXII-specific failure. BNPL options (Klarna/Afterpay) and trust signals are the standard levers.

Source: eMarketer citing Dynamic Yield; Baymard Institute

§ 05·R
Order Relationships

How weekly orders track against the traffic and engagement metrics that drive them. Each chart overlays orders (gold bars) against a second metric (line) week by week, with the correlation coefficient (r) summarising how closely the two move together. r ranges from -1 (move oppositely) through 0 (no relationship) to +1 (move together). These are associations across 16 weeks, not proof of cause.

Orders vs Sessions

r = +0.78 · strong positive
Gold bars: orders (left axis) · Line: sessions (right axis)

Orders and sessions move together closely (r = +0.78) - more traffic reliably brings more orders. This is the strongest of the three relationships and confirms that session volume is a primary lever on order count. The W6 TikTok spike is visible as the joint peak in both series.

Orders vs Users

r = +0.78 · strong positive
Gold bars: orders (left axis) · Line: users (right axis)

Orders and unique users track almost identically to the sessions relationship (r = +0.78), which is expected since sessions and users are near-proportional for VIXII (roughly one session per user). Growing the unique-visitor base is closely tied to order growth.

Orders vs Session Duration

r = +0.12 · very weak / negligible positive
Gold bars: orders (left axis) · Line: avg session duration, m:ss (right axis)

Session duration shows almost no relationship with orders (r = +0.12). Longer average sessions do not translate into more orders week to week - buyers convert regardless of how long the average visitor lingers, and high-duration weeks (e.g. W5, W10) were not high-order weeks. Time-on-site is not a useful predictor of sales volume here.
